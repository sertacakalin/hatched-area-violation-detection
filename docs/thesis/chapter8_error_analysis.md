## 8.1. Purpose of Error Analysis

The headline numbers reported in Chapter 7 — mAP@50 = 0.896 on the
held-out test partition, F1 = 0.842 in the field pipeline test —
describe the *aggregate* behaviour of the system but say nothing
about *when, where, and why* it fails. This chapter therefore
complements the quantitative success story with a structured
analysis of the failure modes that remain.

The goal is threefold:

1. To **categorise** the errors observed across the detector,
   tracker, and state-machine stages so that each can be traced to
   a specific source.
2. To **explain** the root causes of those errors using the
   distribution of the v4 training data and the design choices of
   the temporal state machine.
3. To **prioritise** improvement opportunities that can realistically
   be addressed in a follow-up project cycle.

The analysis combines quantitative evidence (confusion matrices,
per-class metrics, the empirical field test) with qualitative
inspection of representative failure cases drawn from the validation
prediction batches generated by Ultralytics.

---

## 8.2. Types of Errors

Errors in the violation-detection pipeline fall into three
mutually-exclusive classes, defined as follows:

| Code | Name | Where it originates | Operational meaning |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **E-DET** | Detection error | YOLOv8s detector | Vehicle was missed entirely, mis-classified (e.g. car flagged as truck), or located with an inadequate bounding box. |
| **E-TRK** | Tracking error | ByteTrack | Vehicle was detected on individual frames but its track_id was not preserved across the polygon traversal, so the state machine could not accumulate evidence. |
| **E-STM** | State-machine error | Temporal state machine | Both detection and tracking succeeded, but the cooldown / min_frames logic produced the wrong final verdict (either fired on a brief edge-graze or suppressed a real short-duration violation). |

For the *system-level* evaluation reported in Section 7.6.1 and the
field test of Section 8.3.2 the three error sources are observed
jointly as standard true-positive / false-positive / false-negative
counts. The internal breakdown into E-DET / E-TRK / E-STM is
performed by direct inspection of failing frames (Section 8.4).

A fourth, narrower error type, **localisation drift** (E-LOC), is
also referenced in figures throughout the chapter. It denotes the
case where the detected bounding box is internally correct (right
class, right vehicle) but its IoU with the ground-truth box falls
below the 0.50 threshold, downgrading a true positive to a false
negative under the strict mAP definition.

## 8.3. Quantitative Error Analysis

### 8.3.1. Per-class confusion on the validation set

The confusion matrix in Figure 8.1 was produced by Ultralytics at
the end of v4 training on the 1 390-image validation partition.
The diagonal is dominant for every class, but several off-diagonal
patterns are informative:

* **car ↔ truck** mutual confusion: pickup trucks and panel vans
  are the most frequent point of overlap, accounting for the largest
  off-diagonal block.
* **bus ↔ truck**: long box trucks are occasionally mistaken for
  mid-size buses, especially when the rear of the vehicle is
  occluded by other traffic.
* **motorcycle ↔ background**: motorcycles are the class most
  frequently missed entirely; rather than being mis-classified into
  another vehicle class, the detector simply fails to emit a box.

### 8.3.2. Per-class precision–recall breakdown

Table 8.1 summarises the per-class detection metrics on the
held-out test set (695 images, 17 039 instances).

| Class | Test instances | Precision | Recall | mAP@50 | mAP@50-95 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| bus | 242 | 0.881 | 0.856 | 0.900 | 0.819 |
| car | 13 090 | 0.891 | 0.924 | 0.961 | 0.805 |
| motorcycle | 124 | 0.745 | 0.802 | 0.815 | 0.481 |
| truck | 7 110 | 0.834 | 0.855 | 0.910 | 0.741 |
| **overall** | **17 039** | **0.850** | **0.850** | **0.896** | **0.725** |

Two systematic weaknesses stand out:

1. **Motorcycle recall and mAP@50-95 are low.** mAP@50 is 0.815,
   but the strict mAP@50-95 collapses to 0.481, indicating that
   even when a motorcycle is correctly classified, its bounding box
   often fails the high-IoU criterion. This is a *localisation*
   weakness (E-LOC), not a classification weakness, and it is
   consistent with the small pixel area motorcycles occupy in
   high-altitude footage.
2. **Truck precision (0.834) lags car precision (0.891).** The
   driver of this gap is the car↔truck confusion identified in
   Section 8.3.1: visually ambiguous pickups generate truck
   false positives.

## 8.4. Qualitative Error Analysis

Three representative failure cases are documented below; each
corresponds to one of the error types defined in Section 8.2.

### 8.4.1. E-DET case: missed motorcycle in 4K drone view

In the manually verified drone partition (cam10/cam11), motorcycle
recall drops to 0.501 (Section 7.6.3). Inspection of
`val_batch*_pred.jpg` shows that the model fails to emit any box
at all for small motorcycles that occupy fewer than approximately
15 × 15 pixels after letterbox to 640 × 640. The motorcycle is
visible to a human annotator but disappears into the YOLOv8s
feature stride.

Figure 8.5. Validation prediction sample showing a missed
motorcycle in 4K drone footage
(`runs/detect/mobese_v4/val_batch0_pred.jpg`,
compare to ground truth `val_batch0_labels.jpg`).

### 8.4.2. E-TRK case: track break during occlusion

When a vehicle is briefly occluded by a passing bus, ByteTrack may
assign a fresh track_id when the vehicle re-emerges. The state
machine, keyed on track_id, then treats the post-occlusion
appearance as a new vehicle that has not yet accumulated enough
in-zone frames to fire. In the field test (Section 8.3.3) this
failure mode accounts for the single false positive: a vehicle
exited the zone briefly behind an occluder, re-emerged with a new
track_id, and was counted again as a new violation event.

Figure 8.6. (Conceptual illustration; capture and insert from a
test video frame where the same vehicle visibly switches track_id
across an occluder.)

### 8.4.3. E-STM case: brief edge graze suppressed correctly

The state machine requires at least `min_frames_in_zone = 5`
consecutive in-zone frames before declaring a violation. At 60 FPS
this corresponds to ≈ 83 ms of dwell time. Inspection of the field
test shows several vehicles whose bounding-box centroid briefly
crossed the zone polygon for one or two frames due to detector
jitter; the state machine correctly suppressed these as
non-violations. Two real violations whose dwell time was just below
the threshold were, however, also suppressed and registered as
false negatives — the operational trade-off discussed in
Section 8.6 below.

diagram from Chapter 5 if available, or `runs/detect/mobese_v4/labels.jpg`

## 8.5. Root Cause Analysis

Each error type is traced back to one or more of the project's
structural constraints:

| Error | Root cause | Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **E-DET (motorcycle missed)** | (1) Severe class imbalance — only 124 motorcycle instances in test vs. 13 090 cars (75:1 ratio); (2) small pixel footprint after letterbox; (3) limited 4K viewpoint coverage in training. | Section 7.6.2 per-class mAP; Section 7.6.3 sub-population breakdown. |
| **E-DET (car↔truck)** | Genuine visual ambiguity between pickup trucks and small panel vans; community Roboflow labels themselves show occasional disagreement. | Confusion matrix Section 8.3.1. |
| **E-TRK (track break)** | ByteTrack uses motion + IoU only, without an appearance embedding; long occlusions cannot be re-associated. Trade-off was accepted in Chapter 3 in exchange for lower compute. | ByteTrack design (Wang et al. 2022); field-test FP case 8.4.2. |
| **E-STM (threshold trade-off)** | Single tunable parameter `min_frames_in_zone = 5` cannot simultaneously catch brief 3-frame lane-change violations and reject 3-frame detector-jitter edge-grazes. | Field-test FN cases 8.4.3. |
| **E-LOC (motorcycle bbox)** | Detector training set bbox precision is bounded by the manual annotator's ability to draw tight boxes around small objects in 4K footage. | mAP@50-95 collapse in 8.3.2. |

No error class is attributable to overfitting (training and
validation curves in Figure 7.1 track each other), to data leakage
(the test set is fully held out), or to numeric instability
(loss curves are smooth throughout). The remaining errors are
therefore *data and design* errors, not *training* errors.

## 8.6. Sensitivity Analysis

Pipeline-level performance was further tested by varying the
assumption about the tracker's reliability while holding the
detector and the state machine at their measured values. The
resulting envelope, plotted in Figure 8.8, captures the range
within which the field-test number falls.

| Scenario | Tracker P / R | Pipeline P | Pipeline R | Pipeline F1 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Optimistic | 0.97 / 0.94 | 0.771 | 0.756 | 0.763 |
| Mid | 0.91 / 0.87 | 0.723 | 0.700 | 0.711 |
| Pessimistic | 0.85 / 0.80 | 0.675 | 0.644 | 0.659 |

Figure 8.8. Pipeline metric envelope vs. tracker assumption
(`docs/thesis/figures/comparison/16_pipeline_metrics.png`).

The field-test measurement (P = 0.889, R = 0.800, F1 = 0.842,
Section 8.3.3) lies *above* the optimistic envelope. This
indicates that the analytical estimate is conservative — most
likely because the field-test 30-second window happened to
contain mostly easy violations (long-duration cruising rather
than brief edge-grazing) and because no severe occlusion event
broke a track within the sample. The implication is twofold:
the analytical framework remains valid as a worst-case bound,
and a larger empirical sample is required to characterise the
average case (Section 8.12).

## 8.7. Failure Case Categorization

Errors are grouped into five operational categories so that
improvement work can be targeted:

| Category | Example | Affected stage | Frequency |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Small-object failure** | Motorcycle in 4K drone footage | Detector | High (mAP@50-95 = 0.481) |
| **Class confusion** | Car flagged as truck | Detector | Medium |
| **Occlusion-induced track break** | Vehicle behind passing bus | Tracker | Low–Medium |
| **Threshold edge-graze** | 3-frame zone touch ruled non-violation | State machine | Low (by design) |
| **Sub-threshold real violation** | 4-frame lane-change suppressed by min_frames | State machine | Low (≈ 1 in 30 s) |

Categories 1, 3, and 4 dominate the residual error budget; the
others are within tolerance for a thesis-grade prototype.

## 8.9. Bias and Fairness Analysis

This work does not involve human subjects directly and therefore
does not raise demographic-bias concerns. Two forms of dataset
bias nonetheless influence the error pattern and must be
acknowledged:

1. **Vehicle-class bias.** Cars dominate the dataset by a 75:1
   ratio over motorcycles (Section 8.3.2). The model is
   correspondingly biased toward predicting "car" and is most
   uncertain about minority classes. This is mitigated through
   class-balancing augmentations (mosaic, mixup, copy-paste) but
   not eliminated.
2. **Geographic and viewpoint bias.** All footage is from Istanbul,
   from elevated fixed cameras and (for cam10/cam11) drone
   altitudes. The model is not expected to generalise to other
   Turkish cities with different vehicle distributions, nor to
   ground-level CCTV, nor to footage shot from inside a moving
   vehicle.

Both biases were anticipated in Sections 2.6 and 4.11 and bound
the deployability claims in Chapter 9.

## 8.10. Impact of Errors

The downstream effect of each error category in an operational
deployment is summarised below.

| Error category | Operational consequence | Severity |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Missed motorcycle (E-DET, FN) | Violation by a motorcyclist is not logged; the offender escapes enforcement. | Medium — undermines enforcement coverage. |
| Car↔truck confusion (E-DET) | The violation is logged but with the wrong vehicle class. Plate and trajectory remain correct. | Low — enforcement still proceeds. |
| Track break (E-TRK, FP) | The same vehicle is double-counted, inflating the violation log and potentially over-penalising the driver. | Medium — accuracy of the log is compromised. |
| Edge-graze suppressed (E-STM correct rejection) | No consequence; the system is doing its job. | None. |
| Real short violation suppressed (E-STM, FN) | Lane-change violations of < 83 ms duration are missed. | Low — such violations are rare and arguably borderline. |

The empirical operating point (P = 0.889, R = 0.800) means that,
on average, **one in nine logged violations** is a false alarm
and **one in five real violations** is missed. For a research
prototype this is an acceptable starting point; for production
enforcement these numbers would need to improve before the system
could be used unsupervised.

## 8.11. Strategies for Improvement

The error analysis above produces a ranked list of concrete next
steps, prioritised by expected impact-to-effort ratio.

1. **Targeted motorcycle data collection.** Add 500–1 000
   manually annotated motorcycle instances drawn from 4K drone
   footage. Expected effect: mAP@50-95 on motorcycle rises from
   0.48 toward 0.65; minority-class fairness improves.
2. **Tighter tracker for occlusion resilience.** Replace ByteTrack
   with BoT-SORT, which fuses ByteTrack's motion model with a
   lightweight appearance embedding. Expected effect: track-break
   FP rate drops by an estimated 50 %.
3. **Adaptive state-machine threshold.** Replace the constant
   `min_frames_in_zone = 5` with a velocity-aware threshold
   (faster vehicles require fewer in-zone frames to be considered
   intentional). Expected effect: short-violation FN rate drops
   without inflating edge-graze FP rate.
4. **Active learning loop on motorcycle false negatives.**
   Periodically re-evaluate the live deployment, flag low-confidence
   motorcycle detections, route them to Label Studio for human
   verification, and fine-tune. Expected effect: motorcycle recall
   converges to car recall over time.
5. **Higher-resolution inference path for 4K source.** Run the
   detector at imgsz = 1280 on 4K input to halve the small-object
   pixel-footprint problem. Expected effect: mAP@50-95 on motorcycle
   rises further at a 4 × inference-cost penalty.

Items 1, 3, and 4 require no architectural change and are the
recommended first targets for a follow-up project cycle (Chapter 11).

## 8.12. Limitations of the Analysis

The error analysis presented in this chapter is bounded by four
limitations that should be considered before drawing operational
conclusions.

1. **Small empirical sample.** The field test of Section 8.3.3
   spans 30 seconds of footage and 10 violation events. The
   resulting metrics carry a wide confidence interval; a 100-event
   campaign would reduce the standard error by approximately ×3.
2. **Single annotator.** All ground-truth counts, including the
   field-test counts, were produced by the author. No
   inter-annotator agreement was measured. Subtle definitional
   ambiguities (e.g. "is grazing the zone boundary a violation?")
   may therefore systematically shift the reported numbers.
3. **One operating point.** Precision, recall, and F1 are reported
   at the default detector confidence threshold (0.35) and default
   state-machine parameters. A full ROC sweep would reveal the
   precision–recall trade-off available by tuning these knobs but
   was not performed within the project's time budget.
4. **No weather or extreme-lighting cases.** All evaluated footage
   is daytime, clear weather. Error rates under rain, snow, fog,
   or night-time low-light conditions are not characterised and
   may differ substantially from those reported here.

These limitations are referenced again in Chapter 10
(Ethics and Limitations) and Chapter 11 (Conclusion and Future
Work).
